Introduction

Cinema has long been a mirror to society, reflecting cultural shifts, technological breakthroughs, and audience preferences. Over the past century, the film industry has transformed dramatically, from silent films in the early 20th century to the CGI-heavy blockbusters of today. These changes have not only redefined the art of storytelling but also reshaped the business of filmmaking, offering unique insights into what drives a movie’s success. The Evolution of Cinema: The Success Story Behind Box-Office Hits (1915–2015) explores the critical factors that influenced the success of films during this transformative period. By examining key elements such as genre trends, release schedules, star power, and localization strategies, this project investigates how filmmakers and studios have adapted to the changing tastes of audiences and the emergence of new markets. From the golden age of Hollywood to the global dominance of franchises, this study traces the trajectory of cinema as both an art form and a global industry. Through a data-driven analysis of box-office trends spanning 100 years, we aim to uncover the patterns that define cinematic success. What genres have stood the test of time? How has technology influenced storytelling and audience engagement? What role do international markets and localization play in shaping a film’s global appeal? By answering these questions, this project offers a deeper understanding of the intersection between creativity, commerce, and culture in the world of cinema.

Datasets

Our project leverages two comprehensive datasets to analyze the factors influencing box-office success for films released between 1915 and 2015. These datasets provide complementary information about movie characteristics, ratings, revenue, and metadata, enabling us to conduct an in-depth analysis of audience preferences, industry trends, and their impact on box-office performance. Below, we introduce the two datasets we will use:

CMU Movie Summary Corpus

Created by researchers at Carnegie Mellon University, this dataset contains metadata for 42,306 movies, including:

Metadata Provided:

  • Box-office revenue
  • Genre(s)
  • Release date
  • Runtime
  • Language(s)
  • Country of origin

Actor and Character Information:

  • Character names
  • Actor details (name, gender, age, height, ethnicity)

Supplementary Data:

  • Processed summaries using Stanford CoreNLP
  • Named entity recognition (NER)
  • Coreference resolution

Source: CMU Movie Summary Corpus

IMDb Datasets

Comprehensive movie information from IMDb, including:

Key Files:

  • title.basics.tsv.gz Movie metadata, titles, genres, release years, runtime
  • title.ratings.tsv.gz IMDb ratings and vote counts

Analysis Capabilities:

  • Audience ratings correlation with success
  • Localization strategies impact
  • Genre popularity trends
  • Movie reach and revenue patterns

Source: IMDb Data Files

Chapter 0: The Beginning

Challenge:

        At the prestigious École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne (EPFL), nestled in the serene Swiss city of Lausanne, five students shared an unlikely passion: cinema. Although they were studying engineering and science, Lucas, Myriam, Najmeddine, Elyes, and Yassine found solace in their ADA course, led by the eccentric Professor Greenwood, a woman known for her unorthodox teaching methods. One late afternoon, as sunlight streamed through the large glass windows of the Rolex Learning Center, Professor Greenwood gave her class an extraordinary challenge: “Your final project is to explore the evolution of cinema from 1915 to 2015. But don’t just present it—make it extraordinary. Surprise me.”


Brainstorming:

        It was Myriam, a passionate cinephile, who proposed an audacious idea during their brainstorming session. “What if,” she said, her voice brimming with excitement, “we didn’t just study the history of cinema… but lived it?”
The others exchanged skeptical glances, except for Elyes, the group’s tech guru and inventor. His eyes narrowed as he leaned forward. “Actually…” he began, “that might not be as crazy as it sounds.”
Elyes explained that he had been working in secret on a groundbreaking project in one of the university’s lesser-known labs. His invention, called the ChronoPorteur, was an experimental device capable of manipulating spacetime. Initially intended for simulating historical scientific phenomena, he believed it could be modified to allow actual travel through time.
- Najmeddine, the skeptic of the group, raised an eyebrow. “Wait, you’re saying we could actually go back in time?”
- “Theoretically, yes,” Elyes replied. “But there’s a catch. If we interfere with historical events, we could alter the timeline in ways we can’t predict.”
- Yassine, the group’s blockbuster enthusiast and eternal optimist, grinned. “So? Imagine standing on set with Chaplin, or watching Godard debate Truffaut, or even visiting Spielberg during Jaws! This could be legendary.”
- Lucas, the cautious and pragmatic one, folded his arms. “And if something goes wrong? What if we can’t come back?”
- Myriam leaned in, her voice unwavering. “If we don’t take risks, we’ll never create something truly groundbreaking. Isn’t that what cinema teaches us—to dream big?”
After days of debate, the group made their decision. They would use Elyes’ ChronoPorteur to travel to five pivotal moments in the history of cinema. They spent a week fine-tuning the machine, adjusting its parameters to minimize risks.
But as they finalized their plans, a new idea emerged. “What if,” Lucas suggested, “instead of traveling together, we each explore one of the five eras? It would save time and allow us to focus deeply on each period.”
The group exchanged thoughtful glances.

- Myriam immediately claimed The Silent Era (1915–1930), eager to witness the birth of cinema.
- Yassine jumped at the chance to explore The Golden Age (1930–1950), excited to see the rise of Hollywood’s grandeur.
- Najmeddine, intrigued by creative rebellion, chose The Auteur Rise (1950–1970).
- Elyes, fascinated by technological leaps, wanted to dive into Global Blockbusters (1970–2000).
- Lucas, ever practical, took Digital Diversity (2000–2015), curious to analyze the data-driven transformations of modern filmmaking.

- “Alright,” Elyes said, a mix of excitement and nerves in his voice. “Five eras, five explorers. We’ll regroup once we’ve gathered enough data to tell the story of cinema’s evolution.”

With a final review of their plan, the group stepped up to the ChronoPorteur, ready to leap into their individual journeys through time.


The Homecoming:

Later, the ChronoPorteur hummed as the group returned to the lab, each carrying data from their era. Myriam smiled, holding her notes on the Silent Era.
- “We’ve got everything—genres, runtimes, audience insights.”
- Yassine nodded. “From blockbusters to auteurs, it’s all here.”
- Elyes looked at the piles of data and grinned. “Now, let’s analyze it and tell the story of cinema’s evolution.”

Chapter 2: Hollywood's Golden Age

Hollywood in the 1930s and 1940s was a place of unparalleled creativity and spectacle, and stepping into this world was like entering a dream. The Golden Age of cinema marked the advent of synchronized sound and vibrant Technicolor, innovations that revolutionized storytelling and captivated audiences. As the United States grappled with the Great Depression and later World War II, cinema became a powerful escape, offering both hope and inspiration. Films like Gone with the Wind and Casablanca weren’t just entertainment—they were cultural touchstones that reflected and shaped the era’s collective consciousness. My journey began on the sprawling set of Gone with the Wind. The scale of the production was breathtaking, from the elaborate costumes to the meticulously designed sets. Watching Vivien Leigh and Clark Gable bring their iconic characters to life was an unforgettable experience. Later, I attended the premiere of The Wizard of Oz and witnessed the audience’s collective gasp as Dorothy stepped into the Technicolor world of Oz. Judy Garland’s performance of “Over the Rainbow” brought tears to many eyes, and it was clear why this film became a timeless classic. I spent time with studio executives who explained the workings of the Hollywood studio system, which operated like a well-oiled machine. Studios controlled everything from production to distribution, crafting stars and producing films that appealed to the masses. I learned how genres flourished during this time, with musicals, screwball comedies, and film noir offering a mix of escapism and social commentary. Musicals like Singin’ in the Rain lifted spirits with their infectious energy, while noir films reflected the era’s darker undercurrents, exploring themes of morality and existential despair. This period wasn’t just about Hollywood, though. The war effort saw cinema used as propaganda, with films like Why We Fight rallying audiences to support the Allied cause. International cinema also began to gain traction, with directors like Jean Renoir and Vittorio De Sica offering alternative perspectives. As I collected data, I noted how box office trends, audience demographics, and technological advancements shaped the industry. The Golden Age wasn’t merely a period of technological innovation and creative brilliance—it was a time when cinema became a cornerstone of cultural identity. It reminded me that in times of hardship, people turn to stories not just for escape, but for meaning and connection.

Chapter 3: Auteur Rise

As I stepped into the 1960s, the atmosphere in Paris was electrifying. Cinema was undergoing a transformation, moving away from the formulaic productions of Hollywood’s Golden Age toward a more personal and artistic expression. This was the era of the auteur, a time when directors like François Truffaut, Jean-Luc Godard, and Federico Fellini revolutionized storytelling by asserting themselves as the primary creative force behind their films. Inspired by post-war existentialism and countercultural movements, filmmakers began exploring themes of identity, morality, and rebellion. I attended a screening of Breathless, one of the defining works of the French New Wave, and was struck by its unconventional editing and raw, improvisational style. The jump cuts and fragmented narrative felt fresh and exciting, as if the film itself were alive and unpredictable. Later, in a smoky Parisian café, I joined a lively debate between cinephiles discussing the auteur theory and its implications for the future of cinema. Meeting Jean-Luc Godard was a highlight—he explained his belief that films should reflect the director’s personal vision and challenge traditional conventions. My journey continued to Tokyo, where I observed Akira Kurosawa on the set of Yojimbo. His meticulous approach to every detail, from the choreography of fight scenes to the positioning of the camera, was awe-inspiring. Kurosawa’s ability to blend traditional Japanese storytelling with universal themes of honor and survival showed me how auteurs could transcend cultural boundaries. In Italy, I attended a screening of Fellini’s La Dolce Vita, where the dreamy, episodic structure captivated audiences and redefined cinematic narrative. In Hollywood, the influence of the auteur movement was also being felt. Directors like Alfred Hitchcock and Stanley Kubrick were pushing the boundaries of genre and visual storytelling, while method acting by stars like Marlon Brando and James Dean brought a new level of emotional depth to performances. Films like Rebel Without a Cause and 2001: A Space Odyssey reflected the social and technological anxieties of the era. As I gathered data, I noted how narratives became more complex, characters more flawed, and themes more daring. This era transformed cinema into an art form that mirrored the complexities of the human experience. It was a time of rebellion and innovation, where directors redefined the rules and inspired audiences to see the world—and themselves—differently.

Chapter 4: Global Blockbusters

The 1970s marked the birth of the blockbuster era, and landing at the 1977 premiere of Star Wars felt like stepping into a cultural phenomenon. The crowd’s excitement was contagious, and as the Millennium Falcon roared across the screen, I understood why this film redefined success in the industry. The blockbuster wasn’t just a movie—it was an event, a shared experience that brought audiences together on a global scale. I had the privilege of speaking with George Lucas, who shared how he combined elements of mythology, cutting-edge special effects, and a gripping story to create a universe that resonated with audiences worldwide. This era saw the rise of directors like Steven Spielberg, whose film Jaws became the first summer blockbuster, revolutionizing the way movies were marketed and distributed. Later, on the set of Jurassic Park, I witnessed the seamless integration of animatronics and early CGI that brought dinosaurs to life. It was a technological marvel, showing how innovation could elevate storytelling. The blockbuster era wasn’t just about Hollywood. International cinema flourished, with directors like Wong Kar-wai, Abbas Kiarostami, and Satyajit Ray gaining global recognition. Their films offered unique cultural perspectives, enriching the cinematic landscape. In Hong Kong, I watched a martial arts epic starring Jackie Chan and marveled at the choreography that blended action with humor, captivating audiences across continents. As I collected data, I saw how franchises like Star Wars and Indiana Jones dominated the box office, setting the stage for the sequel culture that still defines the industry. Marketing campaigns became more sophisticated, with merchandise and cross-media tie-ins turning films into global brands. But this era wasn’t just about spectacle—directors like Martin Scorsese and Quentin Tarantino used the blockbuster format to tell deeply personal and socially conscious stories. The blockbuster era was a testament to cinema’s power as both art and commerce. It showed me how films could connect people across cultures, creating shared experiences that transcended language and geography. This was cinema at its most expansive, a celebration of storytelling on a grand scale.

Chapter 5: Digital Diversity

Entering the digital era of cinema was like stepping into a world where the possibilities felt limitless. On the set of Avatar, James Cameron explained how motion-capture technology allowed actors to become Na’vi, bringing the world of Pandora to life in stunning detail. Watching the process unfold, I was struck by how digital tools had transformed filmmaking, enabling stories that were previously unimaginable. Streaming platforms like Netflix were just beginning to disrupt traditional cinema, and I visited their headquarters to see how algorithms were reshaping audience consumption. Independent filmmakers at Sundance shared how crowdfunding and digital platforms gave them the freedom to tell diverse and personal stories. Films like Slumdog Millionaire and 12 Years a Slave reflected this era’s growing demand for representation and inclusivity, addressing global issues through unique cultural lenses. Social media had also revolutionized marketing. I observed how trailers went viral, sparking fan theories that shaped narratives before films even premiered. Data analysis revealed how filmmakers used audience insights to craft content that resonated globally. As I gathered data, I saw how the digital age democratized cinema, making it more accessible and inclusive than ever. This era wasn’t just about technology—it was about storytelling evolving to meet the needs of a connected world. From blockbusters to indie films, cinema became a platform for diverse voices, ensuring its continued relevance in an ever-changing landscape. It was clear that the digital revolution had not only transformed filmmaking but had also redefined what cinema could mean to audiences worldwide.
